Neuroplasticity

What is Neuroplasticity?

“Neuroplasticity is the selective organising of connections between neurons in our brains.”

“The brain's ability to reorganise itself by forming new neural connections throughout life.”

In short, neuroplasticity is the brain’s ability to change, adapt, and strengthen connections. It’s not fixed. We can literally re‑shape how we think, learn, and behave.

Why it matters

Understanding neuroplasticity changes how we show up to learn. Studies have found that students who learn about neuroplasticity tend to do better at school—because they see effort as a lever, not a chore.

Instead of assuming intelligence is predetermined, they believe they can learn and achieve, so they practice. And that practice changes the brain. Practice makes permanent, and cells that fire together, wire together. Knowing this keeps me leaning into learning rather than tapping out early.

How I’m engaging with it

When I hit a tough concept (hello, new coding topics), I remind myself: I can learn this. It’ll take focus and reps, but my brain is built to adapt. That shift in mindset is practical and calming.

Ways to increase neuroplasticity

Practice is key, but how we practice matters. I use the mnemonic AGES:

  • Attention — single‑task, reduce distractions, be present.
  • Generation — create something with the idea: code it, teach it, test it.
  • Emotion — connect it to something that matters to you.
  • Spacing — study in spaced sessions; revisit over days/weeks.

Those four make learning stick.

Shaping the brain I want

Neuroplasticity gives us power. Each focused session, each tiny improvement, is literally shaping future me.

Growth Mindset

What it is and why it’s relevant: Growth mindset is the belief that we’re on a learning curve. It’s not “I can’t do this,” it’s “I can’t do this yet.” With practice, I can.

What surprised me: how much the word yet shifts the room. Knowing everyone can learn is liberating—and keeps me persistent.

How I’ll use it: When I’m studying new concepts, I’ll remind myself I’m capable; I just need focus and practice. Progress takes time—and that’s fine.

How this shapes my learning plan

  • Keep focus: single‑task and cut noise.
  • Keep practicing: short, regular sessions with spacing.
  • Build and teach: generate something tangible.
  • Track tiny wins: reinforce the habit loop.

I’m capable of becoming a developer.
